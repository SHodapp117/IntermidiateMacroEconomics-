italy['Year'] = italy['Date'].dt.year
ItalyInflation = np.array(italy['Italy'])

ibond['Date'] = pd.to_datetime(ibond['Date'])
ibond['Year'] = ibond['Date'].dt.year
ibondInflation = np.array(ibond['iBond'])


plt.title('Italy Inflation rate vs Italy Bond Rate ')
plt.xlabel('Years')
plt.plot(italy['Year'], ItalyInflation, label = "Inflation", c= "red")
plt.plot(ibond['Year'], ibondInflation, label ="Bond", c = "blue")
plt.ylabel('Percentage')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

(b): Why do these two series tend to move together over time?

Bonds are issued by the goverment at a set market value rate, that incooperates inflation. As inflation changes, the bonds rate set price is changed to more accuratly match current market conditions. In this example with italy, as the inflation rate fell the italian goverment could offer the new bonds at a lower rate, and because purchasing power was growing, consumers would buy these bonds.

(c): What economic concept corresponds to the difference between these two series?

The principle of convergence seems to make the most sense right here. The element that is converging are intrest rates. Because Intrest rates factor in inflation and provide some small return to the holder of the bond, the bond rate will always chase but never actually converge onto the inflation rate.
